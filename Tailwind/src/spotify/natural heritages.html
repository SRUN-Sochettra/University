About UNESCO

Natural heritage refers to the planet’s most remarkable and irreplaceable elements of the natural environment, including landscapes, forests, mountains, rivers, lakes, wetlands, deserts, coral reefs, and other ecosystems that possess outstanding scientific, ecological, aesthetic, or cultural value. These areas are living testimonies to Earth’s biological diversity, geological history, and ecological complexity, reflecting processes that have unfolded over millions of years. Unlike built heritage, which is shaped by human hands, natural heritage represents the inherent beauty and functionality of the natural world, providing essential habitats for countless species of flora and fauna, regulating climate patterns, maintaining clean water and air, and offering resources that sustain human life. Many natural heritage sites are also deeply entwined with human culture, spirituality, and traditional knowledge, as local communities have long relied on forests, rivers, mountains, and wetlands for food, medicine, shelter, and cultural practices, often passing down ecological wisdom through generations.

Organizations such as UNESCO recognize the global significance of natural heritage through initiatives like the World Heritage Sites program and Biosphere Reserves, identifying areas of “outstanding universal value” to ensure their preservation and sustainable management. Protecting natural heritage is crucial, particularly in the face of growing threats such as deforestation, pollution, urbanization, overexploitation of natural resources, invasive species, and the accelerating impacts of climate change. Failure to conserve these sites not only risks the loss of biodiversity and ecosystem services but also diminishes humanity’s connection to the natural world and deprives future generations of opportunities to learn, explore, and draw inspiration from nature’s wonders.

Natural heritage serves as a source of scientific discovery, education, and recreation, allowing people to study ecosystems, understand evolutionary processes, and witness the intricate relationships that sustain life on Earth. It also supports cultural and economic benefits, including ecotourism, traditional livelihoods, and artistic inspiration. Globally, iconic natural heritage sites such as the Great Barrier Reef in Australia, the Amazon Rainforest in South America, Yellowstone National Park in the United States, the Galápagos Islands in Ecuador, and Mount Kilimanjaro in Africa showcase the diversity, grandeur, and ecological significance of nature’s creations. In Cambodia, natural treasures such as the Tonle Sap Biosphere Reserve, Virachey National Park, and Cardamom Mountains represent some of Southeast Asia’s richest ecosystems, harboring endangered species, rare plants, and vital water systems that sustain local communities and contribute to the country’s ecological balance.

Protecting natural heritage is not merely a matter of conservation—it is a responsibility to maintain the delicate balance of life on Earth. By safeguarding forests, rivers, wetlands, and other natural environments, societies protect biodiversity, ensure clean air and water, support climate regulation, and preserve spaces of wonder, reflection, and inspiration. Natural heritage reminds humanity of the intricate interconnectedness of life, the beauty and resilience of the Earth, and the urgent need to live in harmony with the environment. It is both a legacy and a promise: a legacy of the natural world as it has existed for millennia, and a promise to future generations that they, too, can experience, learn from, and be inspired by the extraordinary diversity and richness of life on our planet. By valuing, protecting, and sustainably managing natural heritage, humanity affirms its role as a steward of the Earth, ensuring that these vital resources endure as sources of knowledge, inspiration, and sustenance for all living beings.
